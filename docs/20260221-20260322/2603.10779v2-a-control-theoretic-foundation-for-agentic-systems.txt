# A Control-Theoretic Foundation for Agentic Systems 

Ali Eslami 1 and Jiangbo Yu 1

Abstract —This paper develops a control-theoretic framework for analyzing agentic systems embedded within feedback control loops, where an AI agent may adapt controller parameters, select among control strategies, invoke external tools, reconfigure decision architectures, and modify control objectives during operation. These capabilities are formalized by interpreting agency as hierarchical runtime decision authority over elements of the control architecture, leading to an augmented closed-loop representation in which physical states, internal memory, tool outputs, interaction signals, and design variables evolve as a coupled dynamical system. A five-level hierarchy of agency is defined, ranging from fixed control laws to runtime synthesis of control architectures and objectives. The analysis shows that increasing agency introduces interacting dynamical mechanisms such as time-varying adaptation, endogenous switching, decision-induced delays, and structural reconfiguration. The framework is developed in both nonlinear and linear settings, providing explicit design constraints for AI-enabled control systems in safety-critical applications. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Artificial intelligence systems are increasingly embedded within feedback control loops across a wide range of ap-plication domains, including autonomous vehicles, robotics, intelligent transportation systems, and cyber–physical infras-tructure. In these settings, AI components are no longer confined to perception or planning layers, but actively influ-ence control decisions during operation. Modern AI-enabled systems can adapt controller parameters, select among control strategies, invoke external computational tools, reconfigure decision pipelines, and even modify task objectives in real time. This expanded role raises a fundamental question: when an AI system is granted authority not only to compute control inputs but also to modify parameters, strategies, tools, architec-tures, or objectives during operation, what type of dynamical system must be analyzed? Classical control theory provides well-established tools for analyzing systems with fixed controllers [1], adaptive param-eters [2], [3], switched systems [4], [5], and hybrid dynamical systems [6]. These frameworks typically treat time varia-tion, switching signals, or structural changes as exogenous or designer-specified. In contrast, modern AI-driven systems may simultaneously adapt parameters, switch policies, invoke external computational tools, reconfigure decision pipelines, and revise task objectives within a single feedback loop. When these decisions are generated endogenously during operation, the resulting closed-loop dynamics combine multiple mecha-nisms whose interaction must be explicitly characterized.

In this work, we focus on runtime agency , namely decision authority exercised by the AI during closed-loop operation. Design-time choices, such as admissible controller families, tool libraries, and governance constraints, are treated as fixed prior to deployment. The objective is to characterize how runtime decision authority alters the dynamical structure of the closed loop and what implications this has for stability and safety. This problem is increasingly relevant across application domains. In robotics, language-model-based agents reason about tasks, select skills, and orchestrate collections of con-trollers [7]. In transportation systems, agentic vehicles adapt behavior in complex and uncertain environments [8]. Broader analyses highlight that increasing agency introduces opera-tional and governance challenges [9], while recent security studies show that agentic decision-making can create vulner-abilities across cognitive, software, and physical layers [10]. Related work on formal methods for safety-critical systems provides correct-by-construction guarantees [11], [12], [13], [14], but typically assumes that task specifications and control architectures remain fixed during operation. At the same time, recent work on LLM-assisted control has explored a variety of integration patterns, including interactive tuning and controller configuration [15], adaptive compensation and parameter adap-tation [16], [17], model predictive control-based decision sup-port [18], specification and controller synthesis from natural language [19], [20], and supervisory coordination in complex systems such as manufacturing and industrial automation [21], [22], [23], [24], [25]. While these works demonstrate promis-ing capabilities, they typically analyze individual mechanisms in isolation and do not explicitly account for the coupled dynamical effects that arise when multiple forms of decision authority are exercised simultaneously. The central premise of this paper is that agency can be interpreted as runtime decision authority over elements of the control architecture . Rather than defining agency in terms of autonomy or intelligence, we characterize it through the variables that an agent is allowed to modify during operation, including controller parameters, controller families, workflow configurations, and control objectives. This interpretation leads to a hierarchy of agency levels, following the conceptual classification in [9], in which increasing authority corresponds to progressively richer classes of dynamical systems. A key implication is that increasing agency changes the structure of the closed-loop dynamics. Parameter adaptation produces time-varying systems, strategic controller selection produces switched systems, architectural reconfiguration leads to hybrid systems, and generative design or objective selec-tion introduces evolving design states. While each of these mechanisms has been studied extensively in isolation, AI-enabled systems combine them within a single feedback loop, 1

C. Definition of Agency 

Agency is defined as runtime decision authority over ele-ments of the control architecture. Let 

q(t) = {θ(t), α (t), c (t), ζ (t)}

denote the decision variables governing parameters, controller selection, workflow composition, and objectives. Agency cor-responds to the subset of q(t) whose evolution is determined endogenously. Signals such as m(t), z(t), and r(t) influence the decision process but are not themselves decision variables. It should be noted that autonomy and agency are related but distinct concepts. Autonomy refers to the ability of a system to generate control inputs without external intervention, given a fixed control architecture. In this case, the mapping from information to action is predetermined, and the system oper-ates independently within that structure. Agency, in contrast, refers to the ability of the system to modify the elements that define this mapping during operation. Specifically, an agentic system can influence the evolution of the decision variables 

q(t) = {θ(t), α (t), c (t), ζ (t)}, thereby altering how control inputs are generated. Thus, autonomy operates at the level of execution within a fixed policy, whereas agency operates at the level of policy formation and modification. A system may be fully autonomous yet non-agentic if its control law is fixed, while a system exhibits agency when it has runtime authority to adapt parameters, switch strategies, reconfigure workflows, or revise objectives. III. A GENCY LEVELS AS HIERARCHICAL RUNTIME 

AUTHORITY 

The framework of Section II defines a set of decision vari-ables q(t) = {θ(t), α (t), c (t), ζ (t)} whose evolution shapes the closed-loop system. The agency hierarchy characterizes how granting runtime authority over these variables alters the resulting dynamics. The hierarchy is cumulative: higher levels inherit the capa-bilities of lower levels while introducing additional endoge-nous variables, switching laws, structural changes, or design dynamics into the closed loop, together with their associated delays. From an AI perspective, this corresponds to increasing run-time discretion . From a control perspective, it corresponds to increasing dynamical complexity : fixed systems, time-varying systems, switched systems, hybrid systems, and hybrid sys-tems with endogenous design evolution.

Remark 1. Theorem 1 shows that Level 2 stability and Level 3 stability do not combine automatically. The adaptation rate ρ

reduces the effective decay margin from γ to γ − Lθ ρ, thereby tightening the allowable switching condition. Faster adaptation therefore requires slower switching. 

Remark 2 (Rationality versus stability) . A decision rule may be locally rational in the sense of always selecting the mode that appears most effective under the current information state. However, if this produces excessive switching while the internal parameter state is still adapting, the closed loop may become unstable. Stability therefore requires regularity constraints on rational agent behavior. 

Remark 3 (Hierarchical MAS and information asymmetry) .

The cross-coupling condition (51) depends on both the adap-tation layer ( ρ, Lθ ) and the switching layer ( ν, γ, τa). In a hierarchical multi-agent architecture, these layers may be operated by distinct agents with no shared internal state. The adaptation layer may evolve θ(t) assuming a fixed mode, while the switching layer reacts to an information state that is itself shaped by adaptation. This creates a circular dependence that neither layer can certify independently. The condition (51) therefore suggests the need for either a shared stability budget across layers or a supervisory coordination mechanism.

From an agentic perspective, cumulative latencies due to reasoning, retrieval, and tool invocation therefore act as stability-critical quantities. Increasing delay necessitates slower switching and more conservative reconfiguration poli-cies, effectively coupling computational latency with control-level decision rates.

The preceding results support a unified reading of agentic stability requirements.

Level 2 requires bounded adaptation rate and controlled inference latency, as captured by Proposition 1.  

> •

> •

Coupling adaptation, switching, delay, and reconfiguration creates stability conditions that do not appear when each mechanism is analyzed in isolation. The adaptation-switching cross-coupling of Theorem 1 and the delay-margin reduction of Proposition 4 are the two principal manifestations of this dependence. The challenge posed by agentic systems is therefore not simply the presence of new decision capabilities, but the need to regulate their joint evolution within the closed loop. 7V. D ISCUSSION 

A. Interpretation and Relation to Existing Work 

The results of this paper provide a control-theoretic inter-pretation of agency in feedback systems. Rather than defining agency in terms of autonomy or intelligence, the proposed framework interprets agency as runtime decision authority over elements of the control architecture . Within this per-spective, the central question is not how the agent computes control inputs, but which structural components of the closed-loop system it is allowed to modify during operation, includ-ing controller parameters, controller families, tool invocation, workflow composition, and control objectives. An important implication is that increasing runtime author-ity changes the class of dynamical system governing the closed loop. Parameter adaptation introduces time-varying dynamics, strategic controller selection produces switched systems, ar-chitectural reconfiguration leads to hybrid dynamics, and gen-erative controller or objective synthesis introduces supervisory dynamics with evolving design states. In this sense, increasing agency corresponds to progressively richer dynamical system classes. This viewpoint differs from classical robust, adaptive, and switched control primarily in the interpretation of the decision variables. In traditional settings, time variation, switching, or structural changes are typically treated as exogenous or designer-specified signals. In contrast, the present framework treats these quantities as endogenous , generated by an internal decision process. As a result, stability must be analyzed not only with respect to admissible signals, but also with respect to the dynamics that generate them. Recent work has begun integrating large language models into control system design and operation, but these approaches differ significantly in the authority granted to the AI com-ponent. Some methods employ LLMs primarily as design-time assistants, translating natural-language specifications into formal control problems or supporting controller synthesis and tuning [19], [20]. Other approaches incorporate LLMs within the feedback loop to influence runtime behavior, for example by adapting controller parameters [16], [17] or coordinating supervisory decisions in multi-agent systems [21], [22]. Table III interprets representative LLM–control frameworks through the proposed hierarchy by identifying which elements of the control architecture are influenced by the AI compo-nent. Importantly, most existing approaches operate at isolated levels of agency, primarily focusing on parameter adaptation (Level 2) or supervisory orchestration (Level 4), while higher levels involving architectural or objective generation remain largely unexplored. Critically, these approaches do not explicitly account for the coupled dynamical effects identified in Section IV, where adaptation, switching, delay, and structural reconfiguration interact to determine stability. This gap suggests that existing LLM-based control frameworks overlook stability constraints arising from the endogenous nature of agent decisions, con-straints that become critical when multiple agency mechanisms operate simultaneously. The framework also clarifies the relationship between agen-tic control and the literature on formal methods and correct-by-construction synthesis. In traditional synthesis approaches, specifications are defined offline and controllers are verified prior to deployment. Higher levels of agency suggest a shift toward runtime synthesis and verification , where an agent may generate candidate control architectures and invoke verification tools such as Lyapunov analysis, sum-of-squares programs, or temporal-logic procedures to certify candidate designs before deployment. While such mechanisms are not developed here, the framework provides a structure within which they can be embedded. In particular, the agency feasibility condition implicit in Proposition 5 suggests that the governance set Gc

at Level 5 can be formally interpreted as the collection of goal descriptors ζ for which a certifying Lyapunov function exists, connecting the agent’s generative authority directly to classical stability certificates.

The analysis of Section IV shows that stability in agentic systems is governed by the joint evolution of adaptation, switching, delay, and structural reconfiguration. A key obser-vation is that these mechanisms do not combine independently: their coupling introduces constraints that are absent when each mechanism is considered in isolation. C. Limitations 

Several limitations of the present work should be noted. The analysis focuses on runtime agency and does not address how admissible controller families, tool libraries, or governance constraints should be constructed at design time. While the main results of Section IV are stated in general nonlinear form, the baseline propositions are illustrated using linear or structured examples for clarity; extensions to broader nonlinear settings follow from standard techniques in the literature. The formal coupling analysis focuses on pairwise interac-tions between mechanisms. In particular, Theorem 1 char-acterizes the interaction between adaptation and switching (Levels 2–3), while Proposition 4 characterizes the interaction between delay and switching. The stability conditions for Level 4 reconfiguration (Proposition 3) are derived under iso-lated operation and do not account for simultaneous adaptation or accumulated delay. Extending the analysis to fully coupled multi-mechanism dynamics across all agency levels remains an open problem. The present formulation also considers a single agent embedded in a feedback loop. Extending the framework to multi-agent systems introduces additional challenges related to interaction, coordination, and information asymmetry, as highlighted in Remark 3, where cross-level coupling con-ditions may not be certifiable by individual agents acting independently. Finally, the effect of semantically ambiguous interaction signals r(t) and the role of interpretation error ∆( t) in driving switching or goal generation are not explicitly modeled, and represent an important direction for future investigation. VII. C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK 

This paper presented a control-theoretic framework for mod-eling agentic AI systems embedded within feedback control loops. In the proposed formulation, agency is interpreted as 

decision authority over elements of the control architecture ,including controller parameters, goal descriptors, tool invoca-tion, and controller structure. By embedding capabilities such as memory, learning, interaction, and tool use into a unified dynamical representation, the framework provides a systematic way to interpret agentic AI behavior using familiar concepts from control theory. The analysis shows that increasing agency changes the type of dynamical system induced in closed loop. Adaptive agency introduces time-varying dynamics through parameter updates. Strategic decision-making produces switched systems through controller or objective selection. Reasoning and tool invocation introduce delays in the feedback loop. Structural reconfigura-tion of the control pipeline leads naturally to hybrid dynamical systems. Finally, generative agency may produce objective-driven policy variation. These mechanisms are individually well understood in control theory, but the present framework shows that they arise naturally when the AI decision process becomes an endogenous component of the feedback loop. In this way, the proposed formulation helps bridge the conceptual gap between emerging agentic AI capabilities and estab-lished dynamical systems analysis, providing a foundation for studying AI-enabled decision architectures in safety-critical cyber-physical systems such as autonomous vehicles, robotic systems, and intelligent infrastructure. Several directions remain for future research. First, the present work focuses primarily on modeling and illustrative analysis. A natural next step is the development of more complete stability and performance guarantees for agentic con-trol architectures, including conditions under which parameter adaptation, controller switching, architectural reconfiguration, or objective updates preserve closed-loop stability. Second, the framework should be extended to multi-agent settings in which multiple agentic controllers interact with one another and with shared environments. Such systems may exhibit coordination dynamics, communication delays, and emergent behaviors that require new analysis tools from distributed control and multi-agent systems. Third, practical design guide-lines are needed for incorporating agentic AI into safety-critical control systems. Examples include limiting adaptation rates, imposing dwell-time constraints on controller switching, bounding decision-induced delays, and enforcing governance constraints on objective or architecture synthesis. Additional directions include studying robustness of agentic controllers under model uncertainty, analyzing the effects of semantic ambiguity in interaction signals, and developing verification and certification methods for AI-driven control architectures.
