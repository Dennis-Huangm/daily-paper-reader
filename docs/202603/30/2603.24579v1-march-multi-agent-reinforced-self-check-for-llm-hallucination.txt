Hallucination remains a critical bottleneck for large language models (LLMs), undermining their reliability in real-world applications, especially in Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems. While existing hallucination detection methods employ LLM-as-a-judge to verify LLM outputs against retrieved evidence, they suffer from inherent confirmation bias , where the verifier inadvertently reproduces the errors of the original generation. To address this, we introduce Multi-Agent Reinforced self-Check for Hallucination (MARCH), a framework that enforces rigorous factual alignment by leveraging deliberate information asymmetry . MARCH orchestrates a collaborative pipeline of three specialized agents: a Solver, a Proposer, and a Checker. The Solver generates an initial RAG response, which the Proposer decomposes into claim-level verifiable atomic propositions. Crucially, the Checker validates these propositions against retrieved evidence in isolation, deprived of the Solver’s original output. This well-crafted information asymmetry scheme breaks the cycle of self-confirmation bias. By training this pipeline with multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), we enable the agents to co-evolve and optimize factual adherence. Extensive experiments across hallucination benchmarks demonstrate that MARCH substantially reduces hallucination rates. Notably, an 8B-parameter LLM equipped with MARCH achieves performance competitive with powerful closed-source models. MARCH paves a scalable path for factual self-improvement of LLMs through co-evolution. The code is at https://github.com/Qwen-Applications/MARCH .

# 1. Introduction 

Despite the remarkable success achieved by Large Language Models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2024; Touvron et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; DeepSeek-AI, 2025; Gemini, 2025), the persistence of hallucinations remains a fundamental barrier to LLMs’ trustworthy usage (Kalai et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025; Ji et al., 2023; Maynez et al., 2020). Even enhanced within Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020), LLMs frequently generate responses that, while linguistically fluent, contradict the provided evidentiary context (Xu et al., 2025c). Such context-conflicting hallucinations (Liu et al., 2024b) are particularly prevalent in data-intensive tasks, manifesting as inaccurate numbers, distorted temporal sequences, or erroneous arithmetic aggregations, even when the source text explicitly contains the requisite information. In high-stakes domains, such as finance (Kang & Liu, 2023), law (Dahl et al., 2024), and healthcare (Kim et al., 2025), hallucinations are more prohibitive, as users require LLMs to function as meticulous evidence readers rather than creative generators of plausible but unverified narratives. Among approaches in mitigating hallucinations, Supervised Fine-Tune (SFT) has been primarily considered as effective (Elaraby et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023). However, several practices emphasize that SFT could also amplify hallucinations by fundamentally operating on behavior cloning (Torabi et al., 2018), which induces models to prioritize authoritative stylistic patterns over factual accuracy (Casper et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2025; Gekhman et al., 2024). Consequently, Reinforcement Learning (RL) has become a necessity for optimizing LLMs towards more truthfulness (Yang et al., 2025; Gemma, 2025). However, the standard outcome-level RL rewards lack the granularity required to supervise fine-grained factual consistency, leading to a fundamental misalignment between the scalar reward and the nuanced evidentiary requirements of RAG. Specifically, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) typically relies on reward models (RMs) (Bradley & Terry, 1952; Li et al., 2025a,b; Liu et al., 2024a) that compress multifaceted evaluations into coarse-grained scalar scores. This loss of granularity limits the ability to verify individual claims, providing insufficient signals to reinforce the meticulous evidentiary grounding required for data-intensive tasks. Alternatively, while Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Reward (RLVR) (DeepSeek-AI, 2025) attempts to provide objective feedback via deterministic rules (Shao et al., 2024), rubrics (Gunjal et al., 2025), or external generalist reward model (Liu et al., 2025b), it remains fundamentally constrained by a dual-dependency bottleneck: the scarcity of expert-annotated groundtruth in specialized domains, and the reasoning ceilings of external verifiers. Consequently, unsupported intermediate claims can persist, as models are rewarded for correct final outcomes despite potentially hallucinated reasoning trajectories (Li & Ng, 2025). While post-hoc metrics and fact-checkers (Tang et al., 2024; Rashkin et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Baker et al., 2025; Guan et al., 2025) provide utility for evaluation, such static tools are seldom integrated into the internal optimization loop to provide actionable, claim-oriented guidance. More critically, the efficacy of such verifiers is fundamentally undermined by the information leakage inherent in current evaluation schemes (Seo et al., 2025). Specifically, concurrent exposure to the query, documents, and generated response induces a confirmation bias (Sharma et al., 2024). In this state, verifiers tend to prioritize validating internal coherence over objective grounding against source evidence, leading to the unwarranted endorsement of erroneous claims. This compromised “blind scrutiny” prevents the model from internalizing the rigorous, document-anchored reasoning essential for high-fidelity generation. To address above factuality challenges, we introduce Multi-Agent Reinforced self-Check for Hallucination (MARCH), an RL-based framework that enforces factual alignment through deliberate information asymmetry. MARCH orchestrates a collaborative pipeline of three specialized agents: a Solver , a Proposer , and a Checker ,to prevent model-based verifiers from taking cognitive shortcuts through confirmation bias. As illustrated in Figure 1, the Solver first generates a response based on the query and retrieved documents. Subsequently, the Proposer acts as a ’Response Atomizer,’ deconstructing the narrative into discrete, verifiable claims. Finally, the Checker validates these claims by re-answering them exclusively based on the source documents, strictly blinded to the Solver’s original output to avoid cognitive contamination. Through this asymmetric decoupling, MARCH transforms the verification process from a circular confirmation of plausible text into a rigorous cross-examination between generated claims and factual evidence. During training, MARCH promotes agentic self-evolution via a zero-tolerance reward mechanism: any discrepancy between the Proposer’s extracted claims and the Checker’s document-based validation triggers a penalty on the entire response trajectory. This encourages the agents to align their outputs strictly with retrieved evidence, effectively internalizing the capability for rigorous, document-anchored statements. Experimentally, MARCH achieves a substantial

Training Details. Training framework is built upon the VerL (Sheng et al., 2025), and we employ Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) (Zhao et al., 2023) for efficient multi-node multi-GPU training. During the training rollout, we utilize the vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023) for efficient sample generation with the temperature of 0.6. We save and evaluate a checkpoint every 20 steps, and report results from the single best-performing checkpoint selected based on the highest average score across all evaluation benchmarks. 3.2. Experiments on Hallucination Evaluation Benchmark. We evaluate MARCH along the aspect of hallucination and factual grounding on the RAGTruth (Niu et al., 2024), FaithBench (Tamber et al., 2025), ContextualJudgeBench (Xu et al., 2025a) and Facts Grounding benchmark (Jacovi et al., 2025), which cover QA, data-to-text writing, summarization, and diverse domains such as medical, financial, and legal. Detailed introductions are provided in Appendix B.2.1. The best performance is marked with Red and the second-best with Blue. 

Evaluation Protocol and Metrics. Following Niu et al. (2024); Tamber et al. (2025); Jacovi et al. (2025); Xu et al. (2025a), we focus on response-level factuality eval. Given generation, retrieved documents, and ground-truth reference, we deploy a Qwen3-235B-A22B (Yang et al., 2025) as a judge model to decide whether the response is factually consistent/accurate with the evidence/ground-truth factual labels. We mitigate the randomness during evaluation by drawing eight independent generations for each test query; the judge model then evaluates each sampling individually, and the final verdict is determined by a majority vote across these eight trajectories.

3.3. Experiments on Multi-Hop QA 

Intuitively, the capacity for precise factual extraction and verification reasonably serves as the fundamental bedrock for complex reasoning, as Question Answering tasks usually require a model to sequentially bridge information across disparate documents. In this section, we empirically find that the improved factual grounding brought by MARCH can directly translate into better performance on reasoning-intensive downstream tasks. 

Evaluation Benchmarks. Following Xu et al. (2025b); Trivedi et al. (2023); Shao et al. (2023), we evaluate the performance of our MARCH on three popular multi-hop QA datasets, including HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), 2WikiMultiHopQA (Ho et al., 2020) and MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022). More details about datasets are provided in Appendix B.2.2. 

Evaluation Protocol and Metrics. Following Xu et al. (2025b); Shao et al. (2023); Trivedi et al. (2023), we sample the first 500 examples from the development set of each dataset as our evaluation subset to reduce the evaluation overhead while maintaining statistical reliability. For each question, the model receives the query and its associated context passages and produces a corresponding answer. We report accuracy (%) with respect to the GT reference answers judged by a Qwen3-235B-A22B, using the official evaluation scripts when available. We also draw eight independent generations for each test query and then adopt a majority voting to decide the final verdict.

3.4. Ablation Study and Further Investigation Performance Analysis of Joint Optimization. Table 5 illustrates that the joint optimization of the Solver and the Checker consistently achieves the highest accuracy across all training strategies in both STEM and General benchmarks. A key observation is that while the Solver-only update provides a base improvement, the inclusion of the Checker significantly amplifies the model’s reasoning capabilities, particularly in complex scenarios. Specifically, in the Few-Shot setting for STEM, the joint model achieves a remarkable absolute gain of 11.6% over the Solver-only version. These results suggest that the Checker’s auditing signal is crucial for leveraging the full potential of advanced prompting techniques like few-shot and CoT , as it effectively mitigates factual drift and ensures logical consistency throughout multi-hop reasoning trajectories. In summary, Table 5 suggests that by internalizing a claim-level auditing logic, MARCH significantly amplifies the efficacy of in-context learning and multi-step reasoning, ensuring that increased model capacity translates into verifiable factual accuracy rather than hallucinated complexity. 

Does our method conflict with other boosting methods? As demonstrated in Table 5, MARCH is highly orthogonal and can be seamlessly integrated with existing performance-boosting techniques, including RLHF, Few-Shot prompting, and CoT reasoning. When applied to the Llama3.1-8B-Instruct baseline, our method consistently achieves significant performance gains across all benchmarks. For instance, combining MARCH with CoT reasoning further elevates the average accuracy on STEM tasks from 50.93% to 59.13%, and on General tasks from 51.00% to 57.80%. Similarly, notable improvements are observed when MARCH is integrated with Few-Shot (10-Shots) configurations, reaching an average of 61.27% on STEM. Table 5 indicates that MARCH does not conflict with traditional scaling or prompting methods; rather, it provides a complementary layer of factual rigor through its asymmetric self-play mechanism, leading to a cumulative enhancement in multi-hop question-answering capabilities. As shown in Table 6, while all RL-tuned configurations significantly outperform the vanilla baselines, ZTR consistently surpasses ERR (e.g., 61.25% vs 55.46% on Llama3.1 joint-training), suggesting that strict logical consistency is more effective for complex RAG tasks. Within the ZTR, the penalty-based scalar ( −1/0) yields superior results compared to the incentive-based ( 0/1) setting, as the former provides a stronger corrective gradient to suppress hallucinations. 

Impact of Reward Scalars We further compare two scalar assignments within the ZTR framework: a penalty-based setting ( −1/0) and an incentive-based setting ( 0/1). The second group in Table 6 on the STEM dataset show that the −1/0 configuration (59.06%) significantly outperforms the 0/1 setting (50.42%). We hypothesize that because factual successes are relatively sparse during early training phases, the 0/1 incentive fails to provide sufficient contrast between diverse erroneous paths. In contrast, the −1/0 setting establishes factual accuracy as the default expectation, providing a robust corrective gradient that effectively suppresses hallucinations. 

Performance on Different Model Family. As shown in the last group in Table 6, the MARCH framework exhibits robust generalizability across different model families, with Qwen3-8B reflecting the same performance trajectory observed in Llama-based experiments. Specifically, MARCH elevates Qwen3-8B to average accuracies of 67.90% and 68.11% under General and STEM settings, respectively, marking a significant absolute gain of over 11% compared to its vanilla baseline (56.84%) and demonstrating that the dual-trajectory optimization mechanism is model-agnostic and consistently internalizes superior evidentiary grounding across diverse architectures.

# 4. Related Work 

Hallucinations and Fact-Check in RAG. While RAG reduces fabrications by anchoring responses in external context (Lewis et al., 2020), LLMs remain susceptible to context-conflicting hallucinations (Shuster et al., 2021). Current mitigation efforts largely revolve around outcome-level supervision (Shi et al., 2023; Gekhman et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024a; Asai et al., 2023), post-hoc reasoning refinements and utilizing external verifiers to filter outputs during inference (Dhuliawala et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023; Min et al., 2023; Rashkin et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024). However, these methods often treat LLM as a black box and fail to internalize factual constraints within the training objective itself. Moving beyond, we propose a framework where the model learns to prioritize factual adherence by minimizing logical discrepancies identified through an internal audit. 

Multi-Agent Systems for LLMs. Multi-agent systems have emerged as a robust paradigm for enhancing LLM performance through collaborative or adversarial interactions (Du et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023), which typically assign distinct personas to one / multiple model instances to facilitate code generation (Huang et al., 2023; Islam et al., 2024), complex reasoning (Li et al., 2023; He et al., 2023; Talebirad & Nadiri, 2023), decision-making (Shinn et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024b), recommendation (Wang et al., 2024b), and debate-driven consensus (Du et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024). While effective at inference time, most existing architectures treat agents as separate entities, often overlooking the potential for joint policy optimization across different functional roles. In addition, previous work shows that multi-agent system sometimes amplifies the hallucination of LLMs (Hong et al., 2023). Our framework differs by instantiating functionally distinct roles within a single shared policy. By structurally enforcing an information barrier between agents, we facilitate the co-evolution of generative and evaluative capabilities within a unified training loop, ensuring that the model’s internal verification logic directly informs its generative precision. 

# 5. Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduced MARCH, a multi-agent framework designed to mitigate hallucinations in data-intensive RAG tasks. By reconfiguring a shared policy into a coordinated hierarchy comprising a Solver, a Proposer, and a Checker, MARCH enables LLMs to simultaneously refine their synthesis reasoning and indepen-dent grounding capabilities. Our experiments demonstrate that the combination of a Zero-Tolerance Reward mechanism and dual-trajectory PPO effectively internalizes a self-contained verification loop, consistently outperforming strong baselines. The robust generalization of MARCH across diverse model families underscores its potential as a scalable, high-fidelity solution for information synthesis in complex knowledge domains. We believe this agentic co-evolution paradigm offers a promising pathway toward building inherently trustworthy and verifiable large language models.

A.2. System Prompt of the Proposer Agent 

You are a data annotation expert. Your task is to analyze a model’s response to a user query. For every critical number found in the model’s response, you must create a question where the answer is precisely and exactly that number. 

Rule 1. Clarity and Specificity of Questions: Each question must be clear, complete, and unambiguous. It must contain enough context from the response to ensure the number is the only possible correct answer. For example, if the model’s response contains “In 2025, 50 people will take the bar exam in Beijing,” an effective question would be “How many people will take the bar exam in the Beijing area in 2025?”, and the answer is 50. A bad example would be “How many people will take the bar exam in 2025?”. This is ambiguous because it lacks the specific region (’Beijing area’). 

Rule 2. Format of the Answer: The answer must be a pure number, either an integer or a decimal. It must NOT include any other characters or formats, such as percentage signs (%), ’k’ to denote thousands (e.g., 10k), ranges (e.g., 10-20), or words (e.g., fifty). Please provide your output as an unordered list using the following format for each question-answer pair: - Question: xxx [Answer: n] - Question: xxx [Answer: n] Now, generate questions for the user query and model response below. Do not output any other content or explanations.

B.4. How Would Proposed Question Numbers Influence Performance? 

A potential reward hacking of the MARCH framework could be that the system may attempt to inflate its reward by reducing the number of proposed questions, adhering to a “less said, less leaked” strategy where fewer claims minimize the probability of being audited as a hallucination. As illustrated in Figure 3, we indeed observe a gradual decline in the average number of proposed questions as training progresses across all configurations. Notably, the configuration involving joint updates of the Solver + Checker exhibits a more pronounced downward trend compared to the Solver Only baseline, suggesting that the zero-tolerance penalty on the Checker’s trajectory imposes a stricter consistency bottleneck, further incentivizing the Proposer to prune potentially risky claims. However, we find that this quantitative shrinkage can be effectively mitigated through simple instructional constraints.
