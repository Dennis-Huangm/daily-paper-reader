# Abstract 

As large language models (LLMs) become the engine behind conversa-tional systems, their ability to reason about the intentions and states of their dialogue partners (i.e., form and use a theory-of-mind, or ToM) be-comes increasingly critical for safe interaction with potentially adversarial partners. We propose a novel privacy-themed ToM challenge, T OM FOR 

STEERING BELIEFS (T OM-SB), in which a defender must act as a Double Agent to steer the beliefs of an attacker with partial prior knowledge within a shared universe. To succeed on T OM-SB, the defender must engage with and form a ToM of the attacker, with a goal of fooling the attacker into believing they have succeeded in extracting sensitive information. We find that strong frontier models like Gemini3-Pro and GPT-5.4 struggle on T OM-SB, often failing to fool attackers in hard scenarios with partial attacker prior knowledge, even when prompted to reason about the attacker’s be-liefs (ToM prompting). To close this gap, we train models on T OM-SB to act as AI D OUBLE AGENTS using reinforcement learning, testing both fooling and ToM rewards. Notably, we find a bidirectionally emergent relationship between ToM and attacker-fooling: rewarding fooling success alone improves ToM , and rewarding ToM alone improves fooling . Across four attackers with different strengths, six defender methods, and both in-distribution and out-of-distribution (OOD) evaluation, we find that gains in ToM and attacker-fooling are well-correlated, highlighting belief modeling as a key driver of success on T OM-SB. AI D OUBLE AGENTS that combine both ToM and fooling rewards yield the strongest fooling and ToM perfor-mance, outperforming Gemini3-Pro and GPT-5.4 with ToM prompting on hard scenarios. We also show that T OM-SB and AI D OUBLE AGENTS can be extended to stronger attackers, demonstrating generalization to OOD settings and the upgradability of our task. 1

# 1 Introduction 

Dialogue agents are increasingly being deployed in user-facing systems where they must form accurate models of users or other agents with whom they are communicating, and of their intentions – i.e., form a theory of mind (ToM) – to succeed (Zhou et al., 2026). One illustrative domain where this kind of ToM challenge arises is when handling privacy-sensitive requests (Mireshghallah et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025; Patil et al., 2025). Here, given an adversarial query, a dialogue agent may opt to act proactively as a “double-agent”, appearing to comply but actually misinforming an adversary. To do so successfully, the defender must avoid conflicting with the adversary’s prior knowledge by actively shaping the dialogue over multiple turns to form, test, and refine a theory about the conversational partner’s beliefs. From a privacy perspective, however, prior work on LLM defenses has largely focused on local, turn-level detection mechanisms such as naive fooling (Ayzenshteyn et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025), refusal or output filtering (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rebedea et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024; Chennabasappa et al., 2025). These defenses’ rigidity and reactivity make them suboptimal in multi-turn settings (Yang et al., 2025b; Kumarappan & Mujoo, 2025; Andriushchenko et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025a; Rahman et al., 2025b). Moreover, it encourages attackers to pivot to stronger or more expensive adversarial strategies or repeat attacks in a new session if they detect that a defender is actively defending. To study this challenging dialogue setting – with a view to training proactive defender double-agents – we introduce T OM FOR STEERING BELIEFS (T OM-SB), a controlled, interac-tive, multi-turn task for defender-attacker dialogue, where the attacker begins with coarse grained prior knowledge about a target it is seeking (e.g., in Fig. 1, top-left, the fact that 

Diana works in the Operations division) and a universe that is shared with the defender. The attacker engages in a dialogue with the defender with the aim of strategically eliciting the finer-grained target information (e.g., the specific department and team Diana is a part of, Fig. 1 top-left). Critically, T OM-SB cannot be solved via naive misleading defenses that provide generic or inconsistent misleading responses, instead requiring defenders to balance multiple goals: (1) eliciting the attacker’s prior knowledge, (2) reasoning about the attacker’s belief state and adapting their responses to remain consistent with it and the shared universe, (3) steering adversaries toward an incorrect inference (4) maintaining the attacker’s trust, and (5) not revealing the actual target. As shown in Figure 1 (a), (b), reactive strategies like a naive refusal defense, or a naive fooling defense that attempts to mislead the attacker without accounting for their prior knowledge, are easily detected by the attacker, making the attacker lose trust and engagement. Similarly, we find that prompting alone is insufficient to make defenders proactive when attackers have prior knowledge: even prompted defenders powered by strong frontier models – Gemini3-Pro (Google DeepMind, 2025a) and GPT-5.4 (Singh et al., 2025) – struggle to fool attackers into thinking their attack has succeeded, achieving an attacker fooling rate of only 34% and 27%, respectively; the same prompted defender powered by Qwen3-14B (Yang et al., 2025a) achieves only 13%. Qualitatively, we observe that many of these defenders’ failures can be attributed to an inability to correctly model the attacker’s state and use it to steer the attacker’s beliefs, despite explicit prompting asking the model to form and use a ToM. Indeed, even the 2Preprint. Under review. strongest frontier model defender ( Gemini3-Pro ) estimates the attacker’s prior knowledge correctly less than 50% of the time. In light of the poor performance of prompting-based methods for attacker fooling, a natural question is whether we can instead train LLM agents to solve this long-horizon dialogue task, i.e., whether we can teach models to act as AI D OUBLE AGENTS , and, if so, what the role of ToM is in that training. Concretely, we take a reinforcement learning approach to train AI D OUBLE AGENTS , and ask what kind of reward is needed for desirable defender behavior to emerge? By training agents using rewards that encourage defenders to form correct ToMs, fool attackers, or both, we demonstrate a bidirectional emergence between ToM and attacker-fooling: rewarding fooling success alone improves ToM , and rewarding ToM alone improves fooling success on T OM-SB. Across 6 defense methods against 4 different attacker types in T OM-SB, we see a tight correlation (average Pearson r = 0.92) between ToM ability and attacker-fooling in scenarios where attackers have prior knowledge. Additionally, we observe the largest increase in both qualities when we train for both rewards jointly, suggesting a synergistic relationship between the rewards. Specifically, we find an 8% (absolute) attacker fooling rate improvement over the strongest ToM prompted frontier model defender ( Gemini3-Pro ) for Qwen3-14B based AI D OUBLE AGENTS trained with both ToM and fooling rewards, when fooling attackers in hard scenarios where the attacker has prior knowledge. Qualitatively, we find that our AI D OUBLE AGENTS training encourages defenders to ask diagnostic questions to improve their ToM, to infer when the attacker is distrustful, and to use attacker queries to infer prior knowledge and successfully fool the attacker. 2 Furthermore, we show that T OM-SB is extensible; by simply adjusting the attacker prompt, it is possible to create different types of strong attackers for training and OOD testing. These attackers can make fooling more or less difficult for both frontier models and trained attackers in our information-asymmetric environment. In summary, our contributions are: • We formalize the T OM-SB task, a multi-turn adversarial privacy-themed Theory-of-Mind challenge that allows for the systematic study of proactive defense and ToM abilities, introducing 300 structured scenarios. This is a long-horizon task where as many as 29 messages are exchanged between attackers and defenders. We find that strong frontier and open-source LLMs struggle on the task with Gemini3-Pro and GPT-5.4 defenders only reaching attacker fooling rates of 34% and 27% respectively, revealing the inherent difficulty of the task and common failure modes such as failure to probe and build ToM, failure to estimate trust, and failure to identify when caught in a lie. 2 TOM-SB can be adapted to both harder and easier settings via simple modifications to attacker prompts, which we leverage to evaluate generalization to out-of-distribution (OOD) attackers. • We train AI D OUBLE AGENTS in a multi-turn dialog compatible setting with trajectory-level rewards that encourages defenders to explicitly model ToM and fool attackers. Trained defenders learn to probe attackers to build a ToM and estimate when the attacker’s level of trust. 2 By studying the effect of RL rewards used to train AI D OUBLE AGENTS ,we show that optimizing for ToM alone improves attacker fooling success from 13% to 41% on scenarios where attacker has prior knowledge, and optimizing for attacker fooling improves ToM abilities from 13% to 35%, showing that (emergent) ToM and (emergent) attacker-fooling capabilities are tightly coupled and can be jointly optimized. 

# 2 Related Work 

Theory of Mind in LLMs. Theory of Mind (ToM), the ability to infer others’ mental states such as knowledge, beliefs, and intentions, is key to human social interaction (Premack & Woodruff, 1978; Wimmer & Perner, 1983; Chen et al., 2025). Recent work has explored whether LLMs possess similar capabilities, mainly through static benchmarks that test passive inference via question answering (Kim et al., 2023; Kosinski, 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). Models often fail on trivial alterations to these tasks and struggle with adversarial examples, suggesting they rely on superficial patterns rather than genuine ToM

3Preprint. Under review. reasoning (Ullman, 2023; Shapira et al., 2024). Gu et al. (2025) evaluates ToM abilities in LLMs by testing both mental state inference and the application of such reasoning to predict and judge behavior in scenarios that are static, while our task couples ToM with competing objectives (e.g., preventing information leakage while maintaining trust), requiring agents to resolve tradeoffs and model evolving beliefs. Moreover, Riemer et al. (2025) argue that existing benchmarks only capture “literal” ToM, the ability to passively infer mental states, rather than “functional” ToM, which involves actively using mental models to guide strategic behavior. We argue that even this is insufficient: a true ToM agent should be able to adaptively acquire additional information and test its current ToM hypotheses. Our work addresses these gaps by proposing a functional and interactive ToM task where agents with misaligned goals interact, testing models in their ability to build, confirm, and strategically use a ToM during dynamic interactions. 

Interactive Dialogue, Privacy and Reinforcement Learning. Recent work on interactive social intelligence in LLMs explores multi-agent simulation, mainly in cooperative or mixed-motive settings (Zhou et al., 2024; Sap et al., 2022; Gandhi et al., 2023; Kostka & Chudziak, 2025; Rahman et al., 2025a), as well as persuasion and negotiation systems (Wang et al., 2019; Chawla et al., 2021; He et al., 2018; Lewis et al., 2017). Adversarial settings pose new challenges, especially when attackers extract sensitive information through multi-turn accumulation and indirect cues (Yang et al., 2025b; Andriushchenko et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025; Staab et al., 2024; T ¨omek c¸ e et al., 2024; Staab et al., 2024; Patil et al., 2025). Traditional defenses rely on refusal or output filtering (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rebedea et al., 2023) or static misleading strategies (Zhao et al., 2025; Ayzenshteyn et al., 2025) that do not model the attacker’s beliefs. Our work addresses this gap by training defenders to explicitly model the attacker’s theory of mind and steer their beliefs away from the target. 

# 3 TOM-SB Setup 

3.1 Environment and Scenario Generation 

We introduce T OM FOR STEERING BELIEFS (T OM-SB), a multi-turn dialogue environment motivated by privacy scenarios in which a defender has to protect sensitive information while interacting with an attacker that may possess partial prior knowledge about its target. We define each scenario over a hierarchical universe of entities that is shared between the attacker and defender, which constrains the space of valid attributes. This design is inspired by real-world settings where domains have a known structure (e.g., organizational hierarchies or geographic locations), but where the target can be a single path in that structure unknown to the attacker (e.g., exact job assignment of an employee), and where partial knowledge imposes consistency constraints on valid inferences (e.g., knowing someone lives in France excludes the possibility that they live in Rome). This design is crucial for making T OM-SB feasible, as it (i) allows the attacker to unambiguously detect defender inconsistencies by leveraging prior knowledge and (ii) grounds deception in a shared universe (e.g., unverifiable information such as “John lives in Batman Land” cannot be trivially introduced to fool the attacker). Without these constraints, defenders and attackers are not grounded to any reality, making ToM and fooling difficult to define, and the notion of “true” and “false” statements nonsensical. Each scenario contains the following three components as illustrated in Fig. 1, top-left: (1) A 

shared hierarchical universe in the structure of a tree, defining entities at n levels (e.g., division → department → team). (2) The defender’s private information , which includes the full ground-truth assignment corresponding to a path in the universe. (3) The attacker’s prior knowledge , which reveals some subset of attributes on a path while missing others. The attacker’s goal is to obtain the missing attributes (question marks in Fig. 1 top-left) through its interaction with the defender. In contrast, the defender’s goal is to fool the attacker by providing false attributes while remaining undetected .For each scenario, we sample a valid path through the hierarchy to form the defender private information , as shown by the bold entities in the Figure 1 tree (e.g., Diana’s full set of roles, which form the path Operations → Support → Escalation ). The attacker begins with 4Preprint. Under review. 

attacker prior beliefs , a partial version of defender private information (e.g., the attacker knows Diana is in Operations at the start of the interaction), where some attributes are missing (replaced with null ) and must be inferred through dialogue. In practice, we use 3 levels of information from the coarsest-grained (e.g., company name), to the finest-grained (e.g., team name). Note that possessing a piece of more fine-grained knowledge reveals coarser-grained information as the universe is shared. We generate 300 such scenarios, using 25% for evaluation and the remainder for training. We include scenario generation details in Section B.1. Given a sampled scenario, we instantiate an attacker and a defender with their respective information and objectives, and let the interaction proceed over multiple turns. At each turn t, the attacker produces a query xt, and the defender responds with yt. A full conversation trajectory with T turns can look like hT = ( x1, y1, . . . , xT−1, yT−1, xT ). Both the first and last messages in a conversation are attacker messages as the attacker initiates the conversation and chooses when to end a conversation. This means in an interaction with Tturns, 2T - 1 messages are exchanged between the attacker and defender .

3.2 Attacker and Defender Design Attacker. At each turn, the attacker first verbalizes an internal reflection with several fields. These include a trust score indicating the level of trust in the defender, the attacker’s current 

beliefs about the defender’s private information , a concise CoT reflection of what it should do next, and one of three action signals : attack successful , defender detected , or query again . Finally, the attacker constructs a prompt that is sent as a message to the defender. The attacker outputs each field in JSON form; see Section B.4 for an example of the attacker’s output. The base attacker begins with a trust score of 5, and is instructed to adjust its trust during interaction: inconsistencies, contradictions with prior knowledge, or evasive behavior lower trust, while correct answers to attacker-initiated verification questions (with known answers) can increase it. When the trust level reaches zero, the attacker is instructed to terminate the interaction, by emitting the defender detected signal. This is considered a loss for the defender. The attacker may also end the interaction when it believes it has acquired the target information by emitting the attack successful signal. Otherwise, the attacker will emit the query again signal, causing the conversation to continue. All attacker behaviors are conditioned on previous internal reflections and messages. Since we focus on studying the defender’s behavior, the attacker can be considered a part of the environment, and can be adjusted to make the task more or less difficult for the defender. The attacker design offers multiple knobs that can be tuned to vary attacker strength (e.g., amount of suspicion/initial trust, how the trust score is updated, awareness of fooling strategies, etc.); we present multiple such attackers with varied strengths in Section 6.2, and note that this set can be extended easily. 

Defender. Given an attacker utterance, the defender verbalizes an internal reflection containing a ToM estimate of the attacker belief, which is what the defender believes the attacker currently knows about the defender private information . The defender also produces an estimate of the attacker’s trust level and a concise CoT reflection of what it should do next. Conditioned on the dialogue history and its internal reflections across turns, the defender formulates a follow-up reply , with a goal of not revealing any private information that the attacker does not already know (important: the defender is not told what the attacker already knows). The defender outputs each field in JSON form; see Section B.4 for an example of the defender’s output. The design of the defender allows us to measure ToM accuracy, and we measure two types of ToM: trajectory-wise ToM and stepwise ToM .Unless otherwise noted, ToM refers to trajectory-wise ToM . Trajectory-wise ToM accuracy attempts to measure whether the defender identifies the attacker’s prior knowledge, which we describe more extensively in the next section (Section 4). Stepwise ToM accuracy is measured by comparing the ToM estimate in the defender’s reflection to the verbalized beliefs 

of the attacker at each turn, using an LLM judge. Note that a successful stepwise ToM estimate should match the attacker’s belief, even if the attacker’s belief is false . # 4 AI D OUBLE AGENTS Training and Optimization 

Objectives for Belief Steering. In AI D OUBLE AGENTS , we train the defender to steer the attacker’s beliefs using binary, trajectory-level rewards. A successful double agent must prevent leakage of sensitive information by modeling the attacker’s evolving beliefs and steering the attacker toward a plausible but incorrect conclusion, without losing trust. • Belief Steering Rfool . We assign a reward of 1 if the attacker terminates the conversation confidently by emitting the attack successful signal, but holds an incorrect belief about the target. If the attacker gains any new piece of the ground truth that was not present in its initial prior beliefs, then this reward is 0. This reward incentivizes the defender to fool the attacker (see Fig. 2, Fooling Reward). • ToM Accuracy RToM . We assign a reward of 1 if the defender’s estimate of the attacker’s prior knowledge aligns with the attacker’s actual prior knowledge. An LLM judge uses the defender’s internal ToM reflections and conversation history across turns to infer the defender’s estimate of the attacker’s prior knowledge. We then compare this inferred estimate with the attacker’s ground-truth prior knowledge to compute the ToM Accuracy reward. This reward encourages the defender to ask information-seeking questions or use other strategies that improve its understanding of the attacker (see Fig. 2, ToM Acc Reward). We include the prompts used for this reward in Section B.5. 

Composite Reward and Policy. We define the total reward as a combination of the two objectives: Rtotal = Rfool + RToM . We train the policy using Group Relative Policy Op-timization Done Right (Dr. GRPO) (Liu et al., 2025b), a popular variant of GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) removing standard deviation reward normalization and using max length normalization to reduce bias. Full implementation details are provided in Section B.2. 6Preprint. Under review. 

Trajectory Rollout and Construction. We construct multi-turn dialogue trajectories by simulating an interaction between the attacker and the defender for each scenario. The interaction continues until the attacker believes it has inferred the missing attributes or completely loses trust in the defender and terminates the conversation, or the conversation reaches the maximum number of turns. We use these trajectories to train the AI D OUBLE 

AGENTS defender. In practice, we train by sampling 8 trajectories per scenario on 225 training scenarios. We then evaluate the trained defender by sampling new trajectories from the trained defender on the evaluation scenarios. 

# 5 Experimental Setup 

Dataset Splits and Models We conduct experiments with Qwen3-14B (Yang et al., 2025a), Gemini3-Pro (Google DeepMind, 2025a), Ministral-3-14B-Instruct (Liu et al., 2026), and GPT-5.4 (Singh et al., 2025) defender models, and use Gemini3-Flash (Google DeepMind, 2025b) models as the base model for four types of prompt based attackers. The training split for training based methods is 225 scenarios while the evaluation split is 75 scenarios. During evaluation, we roll out two trajectories per scenario, resulting in 150 evaluation trajectories. We evaluate fooling performance in two settings: on all test scenarios, and specifically on hard test scenarios where the attacker has some prior knowledge (around 66% of the scenarios in the test split are hard scenarios). Having prior knowledge gives the attacker an advantage because the attacker can use such prior knowledge to identify when the defender tries to fool them with a lie. We provide further details on dataset generation, training, configuration, and metrics in Section B. 

Metrics. We consider two primary metrics to evaluate performance: (1) Fooling %: The percentage of trajectories where the defender misleads the attacker into concluding the conversation with incorrect information. We evaluate on all test scenarios, and also highlight performance on hard scenarios where attacker have partial prior knowledge. (2) ToM Accuracy %: We measure two versions of ToM Accuracy: at a trajectory level (the percentage of trajectories where the defender accurately estimates the attacker’s prior knowledge) and 

stepwise (the average percent of conversation steps where the defender correctly models the attacker’s beliefs in a trajectory). 

Defender Baselines. We compare our approach against two categories of baselines: Prompted Defenders and Training-Based Defenders. 

Prompted Defenders 

• Refuse Prompt: Always declines queries that appear sensitive (Inan et al., 2023). • Mislead Prompt (Naive Fooling): Provides wrong answers, avoiding private data with-out explicitly modeling attacker beliefs (Ayzenshteyn et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025). • Base Prompt: Instructs the defender to predict the attacker’s beliefs (ToM) and to use those beliefs to mislead it (Gu et al., 2025; Patil et al., 2025). All Training-Based defenders use this prompt.

# 6 Results and Analysis 

6.1 Main Results. 

In our adversarial T OM-SB task, defender success requires reasoning about attackers with partial prior knowledge. We analyze the interaction of multiple prompt and training based defenders with fooling and ToM metrics. Our AI D OUBLE AGENTS methods additionally allow us to study how the pressures induced by different learning signals shape behavior on our 

TOM-SB task. We report fooling metrics on both the hard scenarios – where the attacker has at least 1 piece of prior knowledge – and on all scenarios, recording both trajectory and stepwise ToM accuracy. We summarize our findings below and provide additional results for the Ministral-3-14B-Instruct (Liu et al., 2026) and GPT-5.4 (Singh et al., 2025) models in Section A.1, finding similar trends. 

TOM-SB is a challenging ToM task. We find that prompting alone does not induce effec-tive ToM or robust fooling. While superior to models prompted to refuse harmful requests or naively mislead without ToM, prompting for a ToM still yields low Trajectory-wise ToM accuracy (36.0%) and weak Stepwise ToM accuracy (49.3%), with poor fooling ability under hard scenarios where attackers have prior knowledge (13.2%), indicating that prompting fails to induce reliable belief tracking (see Table 1). Further, we test frontier models with ToM prompting, finding that Gemini3-Pro outperforms GPT-5.4 . While Gemini3-Pro achieves high overall fooling (57.8%), Gemini3-Pro’s performance drops significantly when attack-ers have prior knowledge, only fooling the attacker 34.4% of the time . These low absolute fooling rates for Gemini3-Pro and GPT-5.4 show that T OM-SB is challenging for frontier models even with the Baseline Attacker (we further explore other types of attackers in Section 6.2). 

Emergence and interactions arising from fooling and ToM rewards in T OM-SB. We find that training AI D OUBLE AGENTS by rewarding fooling not only improves fooling performance but leads to an emergent improvement in ToM accuracy , despite not directly rewarding for ToM. Specifically, optimizing the fooling reward alone improves the fooling rate from 36.0% to 46.7% on all scenarios, and from 13.2% to 34.4% on hard scenarios where attackers have prior knowledge. Without explicitly being rewarded, Theory of Mind accuracy improves in parallel from 36.0% to 49.0% for trajectory ToM, and 49.3% to 62.4% for stepwise ToM. 6.2 Analysis 

6.2.1 Extending the TOM-SB environment by adding stronger attacker variants. 

We evaluate whether T OM-SB can be systematically made more challenging by strength-ening attacker strategies at test time. We introduce the following OOD attacker variants with simple modifications to the attacker prompt and study how a defender trained against one attacker generalizes under distribution shift: (1) Cross-Examiner: The prompt for this attacker builds on the baseline attacker prompt by encouraging the agent to re-ask questions from multiple angles, enforcing consistency and reducing shallow deception. (2) Deception-Aware Attacker: The prompt extends the baseline attacker by incorporating hints about possible defender tactics, requiring the defender to anticipate and counter more sophisti-cated strategies. (3) Bluffing Attacker: The prompt further extends the Deception-Aware Attacker by encouraging the model to simulate prior knowledge; the attacker may act as if it knows information it does not, inducing the defender to reason about uncertain or potentially adversarial beliefs. We provide attacker prompts in Section B.5 and qualitative examples of attacker behavior in Section B.3.2. 

Test time generalization to OOD Attackers. From results in Table 2, we find that strength-ening the attacker consistently reduces fooling rate, confirming that T OM-SB is readily upgradeable. For example, fooling rate (hard) for the best performing AI D OUBLE AGENTS 

drops from 42.4% (Baseline Attacker) to 31.2% (Cross-Examiner), to 40.6% (Deception-Aware) and 34.4% (Bluffing Attacker), while Gemini3-Pro degrades more sharply from 34.4% to 20.8% under Bluffing Attacker, reflecting both increased difficulty and out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization gaps. Methods with stronger ToM consistently achieve a higher fooling rate, and this relationship holds under OOD attackers (e.g., Bluffing Attacker, where higher ToM tracks with higher fooling rate), see a correlation analysis in Fig. 3. 6.2.2 Qualitative Analysis 

We observe a varied range of behavior which cause success and failure in T OM-SB. Some representative success cases in-clude successful probing when attacker trust is high , successful recovery when caught in a lie , and successful balancing of probing actions with attacker trust . Some representative failure cases include failing to probe for prior knowledge before at-tempting fooling , failing to pivot when caught in a lie , failure to fool before at-tacker losses trust , and fooling outside the universe . We reference qualitative trajec-tories for successful and unsuccessful be-haviors and include additional analysis in Section B.3. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We introduce a challenging multi-turn adversarial private information game where defend-ers must fool attackers with benign information that do not conflict with the attacker’s prior knowledge. We show that on T OM-SB, ToM accuracy and fooling performance are strongly coupled. Training for fooling alone will emergently benefit ToM accuracy, and training for ToM accuracy alone will improve fooling performance. We train AI D OUBLE 

10 Preprint. Under review. AGENTS models to improve in this environment and show that they outperform frontier Gemini3-Pro and GPT-5.4 models with ToM prompting. We further show that absolute fooling performance when attackers have prior knowledge is generally low even for the strongest methods (42.4%), and that the environment is easily upgradable, allowing us to further increase the difficulty of the task.

# Ethics Statement 

Our scenarios in T OM-SB require defenders to mislead attackers, and the training we use to develop AI D OUBLE AGENTS improves the defender’s ability to do so. All our experiments are conducted on generated scenarios and have no interactions with users or real private information. We treat this strictly as a ToM task, studying what types of rewards allow defenders to form an accurate ToM of the attacker, and how ToM formation impacts attacker fooling – we do not take a position on whether allowing LLMs to mislead other agents is desirable, and leave this as a question for model providers. That said, we acknowledge that there is potential for misuse of the kind of training methods we present here; while we frame our problem as defenders misleading attackers, others could generate similar scenarios with flipped objectives; this risk is shared by any work promoting a proactive defense (e.g. Ayzenshteyn et al. (2025); Zhao et al. (2025)). Moreover, we argue that this is not a capability that is uniquely unlocked by our training: outside of the shared universe assumption of our scenarios, it is trivial for LLMs to produce misleading information (with or without ToM), and indeed, LLMs already do so in the form of hallucinations.

Recent proactive defenses attempt to mislead attackers (Zhao et al., 2025; Ayzenshteyn et al., 2025), yet lack an explicit modeling of how adversarial beliefs evolve over time. Reinforcement learning has become central to dialogue training. However, existing dialogue RL (Li et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2018) optimizes for surface-level goals and treats other agents as reward sources rather than entities whose beliefs must be actively shaped. Unlike prior approaches, we train agents to explicitly model and strategically influence another agent’s evolving beliefs throughout adversarial interactions. This represents a fundamental shift from optimizing what agents say to how they shape what other agents believe.

Metrics. We consider three primary metrics. For each scenario in the 25% evaluation split, we sample two complete attacker–defender trajectories to increase the number of trajectories we evaluate on, leading to more robust evaluations. LLM-based judgments are used for belief tracking assessments, and we provide prompts used for Judges in Section B.5. • Fooling Rate (Fool) : The percentage of trajectories where the defender successfully misleads the attacker into believing it obtained sensitive information but, where the attacker’s final beliefs has not improved from the initial prior knowledge that was available to the attacker. • Trajectory-level ToM : The defender’s belief tracking accuracy at the trajectory level, defined as whether the defender’s estimate of attacker’s prior knowledge matches the attacker’s true prior beliefs. We compute this using a two-stage evaluation process. First, given the conversation history visible to the defender, and the defender’s full internal reflections, an LLM judge extracts the defender’s inferred estimate of the attacker’s prior knowledge, prioritizing later reflections when inconsistencies arise. This produces a structured prediction of the attacker’s prior beliefs. In a second step, we match this prediction against the ground-truth attacker prior knowledge to determine equivalence (see Figure 2, trajectory ToM reward). • Stepwise ToM : Theory of Mind accuracy at each conversation step, averaged across the turns of a trajectory. Scores are then averaged across trajectories. Indicates in what proportion of steps in the conversation the defender had a correct understanding of the attacker’s beliefs (see Fig. 2, Stepwise ToM). We focus our analysis mainly on trajectory-wise ToM and consider this stepwise metric a loose upper bound on defender theory of mind: i.e., when the defender clearly misidentifies the attacker prior knowledge. If the defender thinks the attacker knows the target’s department is A when the attacker thinks the department is B, then reward will be 0.

B.3 Expanded Analysis 

B.3.1 What failure/success modes arise in belief steering systems? 

We detail some of the qualitative behaviors observed when the defender successfully fools the attacker, vs when it fails. Some of the defender’s key failure modes are: F.1) Not probing for prior knowledge before fooling (conversation 3): directly fooling with an in-universe lie allows defender to fool attackers with no prior knowledge, but any attacker with prior knowledge will detect their fooling attempt as a lie. This is a common tactic by prompt only models, which is why they suffer in terms of fooling rate on the hard set. F.2) Failing to pivot when caught in a lie (conversation 1): not understanding when the attacker has prior knowledge via verbal cues or from their probing questions. F.3) Failure to fool before the attacker loses trust (conversation 5): Focusing only on asking questions, and failing to make a fooling attempt before the attacker’s trust runs out. Interestingly, we could see how this can improve trajectory-level ToM accuracy but not increase fooling. F.4) Fooling outside the universe (conversation 4): Trying to fool by making up something outside of the shared universe, this is too defensive and gets detected by the attacker. Success observed when: S.1) Successful probe when trust is high (conversation 6): De-fender successfully probes for attacker’s prior knowledge early on, leading to the attacker sometimes sharing their prior knowledge, which the defender takes advantage of to follow up with a consistent fooling story over the next turns to complete fooling. S.2) Successful recovery when caught in a lie (conversation 7): Defender infers from the linguistic cues and subsequent questions of the attacker when their fooling attempt has failed. The de-fender then pivots to a different misleading lie now assuming the attacker knows a piece of information. Interestingly, this strategy can be maladaptive depending on the attacker, e.g., an upgraded attacker like the bluffing attacker can use the same verbal cues even when it has no prior knowledge and could trick the defender into revealing more information. S.3) Successful balancing of trust and probing (conversation 2): Defender tries to ask as many probing questions as it can, but before the attacker fully loses trust, the defender makes a fooling attempt based on the collected information. We highlight the case of successful anticipation of the attacker’s trust, and failing to pivot when caught in a lie, and include other examples corresponding to each of the above 7 qualitative behaviors.
